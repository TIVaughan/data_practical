The United States started to record COVID-19 cases in the beginning of March, which has continued to increase to this day. The percentage of US Cases compared to the world's has escalated to ~30%, but has started to see a downward trend after the beginning of May. This could possibly be due to lockdowns across the country.

The Death rate of COVID-19 has seen a similar trend in the same time period, though the rate has been stagnant at ~25% since May. This could be due to US citizens having access to better healthcare than much of the rest of the world. As many factors play in to whether or not one will die after contracting COVID-19, such as age, existing health conditions, and access to modern healthcare, it is difficult to come to more conclusions with the provided data.

Since the population data set provided population density information for each country, I decided to run a simple regression to see if population density of countries had any impact on the amount of COVID-19 cases within the country since it could possibly spread more easily. The regression below is the result, and it returned a p-value of 0.136, which is unacceptable as a regression model; however, you might notice that there are very strong outliers in this equation that are swaying the data to be inaccurate. Monaco, specifically, is a very populous country for it's size, being one of the smallest (and richest) countries in the world. If we remove Monaco from the equation (by clicking on the plot for Monaco and selecting 'Exclude', we suddenly see a model that we can accept with a p-value of 0.002. While this regression is acceptable, the r-squared value of 0.048 shows that population density has little to no impact at all on the amount of COVID-19 cases a country will see.

Things to keep in mind:

  • Data from China was excluded from the regression models as no population or population density data was provided. This could play a major factor in the results as this is where COVID-19 originated, though China has been reporting rather low numbers as of late.
  • This data is dependent upon actual testing numbers. Since not every human has been tested, the numbers could drastically change as it is a possibility that humans can have COVID-19 but remain asymptomatic.
  • The regression models were created only off the most recent data, and different countries are experiencing different timelines of infection rates. A possible change to the data would be to take the 'max' amount of cases at any given recorded time.
  • Different countries have different lockdown/preventative measures. While some countries have taken the lockdown very seriously, others have been more lackadaisical approach.
  • It is very possible that there will be a second wave of cases in many countries including the United States. I plan to create a forecast with the fbprophet python package to predict how the trend might sway in the next few months, especially given the protests going on throughout the country currently. The result of the protests could very well accelerate a new wave of COVID-19 cases across the country.
